01 — OVERVIEW
Revenue · Sentiment · Operations

REVENUE, SENTIMENT,
AND WHAT TO DO ABOUT IT.

The data suggests a common gacha pattern: a large launch peak, a high 2024 plateau, and a sustained decline through 2025–26, with temporary recoveries around anniversaries and collaborations. Player sentiment moved on a separate track — driven less by generosity than by perceived roster balance and story pacing.

A note on what this is built from, because it shapes how much weight each claim can bear: the inputs are third-party mobile revenue estimates, public sentiment, and HoYoverse's own announcements. That's enough to describe what happened and to form hypotheses about why — not to prescribe fixes with confidence. Observations here are stated plainly; causes are flagged as guesses of varying quality.

05 — ANALYSIS
What the data can and can't tell us

FOUR PERIODS

Four rough periods, read in sequence. Where a cause is inferred rather than shown, it's flagged as a guess — and a couple of these sections end without a clean recommendation, because from outside the studio the evidence doesn't support one.

1.xAPR–DEC 2023

LAUNCH AND THE PLATEAU BASELINE

Revenue peaked at launch (~$245M gross in 30 days; $500M by month three) and settled into a high baseline rather than falling off a cliff. The v1.6 free 5★ Dr. Ratio landed as a goodwill gesture — and it's around here that the "HSR is generous relative to Genshin" framing took hold in community discussion.

$132.3M (30d) → $500M (25 Jul) → free-5★ goodwill

The detail worth holding onto is that content-supply and endgame-cadence complaints show up even in this stretch, when the numbers were at their best. The friction later attributed to decline was already audible at peak revenue — which argues against reading it purely as a symptom of falling money.

If there's an implication, it's a soft one: endgame and permanent-progression supply may be worth treating as a retention question early rather than a late-game patch. But that rests on how players actually churn, which we can't observe from revenue estimates.

2.xFEB–DEC 2024

PENACONY — THE REVENUE PLATEAU

2024 was the strongest full year on record ($870.9M mobile, ahead of Genshin). The two data points we can actually attach to specific banners both land here: Acheron (2.1) coincides with the ~$92.47M March reading, and Firefly (2.3) was widely reported as a high-demand banner. Past that, per-banner attribution runs out, so most of what follows is inference.

$92.47M reading · $870.9M year · DAU 3.33M

The pattern worth flagging is that powercreep discussion moved from occasional to recurring across the year — and did so while revenue was strong. The two rose together rather than trading off. One reading is that monetisation was leaning on each marquee unit raising the damage ceiling, with a 4★ like Gallagher buffering budget players unevenly. That's a plausible story, not a demonstrated one: we can see the banners and the sentiment, not the pull-rates behind them.

The question this raises — and one an internal team could actually answer — is whether the story-driven patches held revenue as well as the power-driven ones. If narrative peaks can carry a plateau without a power jump, that changes what "content" has to do. From outside, the two can't be separated.

3.xJAN–DEC 2025

AMPHOREUS — SPIKES ON A DECLINING BASELINEfriction

Revenue and sentiment diverged most noticeably during the Amphoreus cycle. v3.0 drew documented feedback on lore density and puzzle complexity, enough that HoYoverse issued a public response on 5 Feb 2025 committing to skip options and pacing changes [R12][R13] — one of the few points on the timeline where cause and effect are on the record rather than inferred. Around the same time, roster economics tightened: Amphoreus shipped no new 4★ units, which community analysis tied to greater premium-team dependence [R16].

And yet the year's two biggest readings came from inside this same cycle — the 2nd anniversary (3.2, ~$71.1M) and the Fate collaboration (3.4, $78.8M). So the picture isn't "sentiment soured and revenue followed." It's that event spikes and a softening baseline coexisted, pointing in opposite directions — harder to act on than a clean decline would be.

$71.1M anniv. · $78.8M collab · ~$2B lifetime

The temptation is to explain the softening baseline with the roster changes: no budget units, Remembrance-path powercreep, endgame tuning that may have assumed access to the newest units. That last part is a hypothesis, and it should stay one — MoC clear compositions aren't visible from here. So rather than "re-tune the endgame," the honest next step is to look at clear-rate data and check whether newer premium units are in fact over-represented among successful teams before concluding anything.

Carry forward
The one thing that seems safe to carry forward is procedural, not mechanical: the v3.0 feedback-and-response loop worked, and is worth repeating as a template when friction appears. That's an observation about process — which we can see — not about tuning, which we can't.
4.xFEB–JUL 2026

SUSTAINED DECLINE, COLLAB-DEPENDENT RECOVERYfriction

Monthly readings step down through the first half of 2026 ($58.1M → $38.8M → $28.4M) with MAU already down around 19.4M by Dec 2025. On its own that's an unremarkable shape for a live-service game several years past its spending peak; the more specific thing is where the recoveries come from. Recent revenue recoveries coincide primarily with collaboration patches, while non-collaboration updates have generally continued the broader downward trend. Extending the Fate collaboration into 4.4 repeats the 3.4 move of using an outside IP to lift a falling baseline.

$58.1M → $38.8M → $28.4M (Apr–Jun ’26)

Why the baseline is falling is where visibility runs out. DAU contraction and the end of the long Amphoreus arc are on the observable side; higher acquisition costs and stiffer competition for player spend are plausible contributors we simply can't measure from public data.

What would help here is less a recommendation than something to watch. If the gap between collab and non-collab patch revenue keeps widening, the baseline is the problem, and permanent-progression rewards and core-content pull-value are the levers most worth examining — provided internal retention data agrees. If instead the whole level is drifting down regardless of patch type, that's a different and harder problem, and worth diagnosing before spending against it.

Taking stock

TAKING STOCK

Rather than restate the sections, three things worth carrying out of this — one that surprised, one we're unsure of, one to chase down.

MOST SURPRISING

Sentiment friction was audible from the very beginning — during the highest-revenue stretch of the game's life, not just on the way down. The instinct to read balance and pacing complaints as symptoms of decline doesn't survive the timeline; they predate it. Whatever drives sentiment appears to run at least partly independent of how the money is doing.

LEAST SURE OF

Nearly every causal claim about why the baseline is falling. We can see revenue estimates, public sentiment, and announcements. We cannot see pull-rates, retention curves, clear-rate compositions, or acquisition costs — the data that would turn these hypotheses into findings. The confident-sounding parts of this report are the observations; the causes are guesses of varying quality.

WORTH INVESTIGATING NEXT

Three checks an internal team could actually run:

  • Whether story-driven patches hold revenue comparably to power-driven ones — the plateau year is well-suited to answer this.
  • Whether newer premium units are over-represented in successful endgame teams, which would show whether the powercreep complaints reflect real tuning pressure or just noise.
  • Whether the collab / non-collab revenue gap is widening, which distinguishes a defensible baseline from a drifting one.

The v3.0 feedback loop is the one piece of process here that demonstrably worked — and the cheapest thing to repeat.
